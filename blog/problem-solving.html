Tell your non-tech friend a story about a time you:

Were blocked on a simple problem:
  • During the Grade book challenge on Kata, I found myself grappling with the task of getting two functions to work together. The first function was needed for calculating the average score from multiple test results, while the second function was needed to locate a student's score that was situated elsewhere inside an object and pass it to the first function.
  • At a certain point, I realised that I was hitting a roadblock and wasn't making any progress. It was clear that I needed to step away for a moment. So, I decided to take a break, disconnected from my screen and went for a walk. I've found that this change of scenery and mental pause can work wonders for changing my thought process and providing me with the clarity needed to tackle coding challenges.
  • Frustration and anxiety tend to creep in when I'm stuck, and it's crucial for me to recognise when I've reached that point and what to do about it. Over time, I've been getting better at this, understanding that self-awareness of this is just as vital as technical skills.
  • Reflecting on this experience, I've gained a deeper understanding of recognising when I'm stuck. It's not just about trying different coding approaches; it's also about acknowledging when I might need to reposition myself mentally and take a break. This insight has proven invaluable in my coding journey.
    • Elegantly solved problem:
  • While I was working on the deBee challenge, I needed to find a solution to remove the word ‘buzz’ that was used in multiple strings. The solution needed to remove not one, but all of the strings containing ‘buzz’ regardless of upper or lower case characters. I was able to write the parts of the code I knew I needed and then I successfully researched a solution and implemented it. I was able to do this quite quickly and it felt good.
  • While I was doing this challenge I utilised taking small breaks and the rubber duck method to keep a good understanding of what I was doing and what I needed to do next.
  • I took influence from my work on previous challenges and went in knowing I would need to research and be constructive in my approach to solving the problem.
  • I feel this challenge was a big part of me coming away with a far better understanding of the inner workings of functions built to process information. I felt a lot more confident in my ability after this and excited to continue on and get better
    • Reflect on how confident you feel using each of these problem-solving methods:

    Pseudo code:
  • When it comes to tackling challenges in my learning journey, I've discovered that writing things down isn't always my strong suit. However, there are times when it proves invaluable, especially when I need a quick way to capture ideas and have them readily available as reminders.

    • Trying something:
  • Trying out different approaches after taking a break is a pivotal part of my problem-solving process. I've developed confidence in this method, and I genuinely enjoy the creative aspect it brings to my problem-solving efforts.

    • Rubber Ducky method:
  • The Rubber Ducky method is a personal favourite of mine. Explaining an issue out loud to myself seems to trigger new angles of thought and aids in mentally digesting the problem I'm facing.

    • Reading error messages:
  • Reading error messages is another essential step for me. It helps me pinpoint any incorrect syntax in my code and fosters a better understanding of the issues at hand.

    • Console.logging:
  • Console logging is a tool I enjoy. Its ability to validate the correctness of code execution significantly expedites the coding process and boosts my confidence in my work.

    • Googling:
  • At this point, Google has become an important tool in my problem-solving endeavours. The vast array of resources available empowers me and, with time, I've honed my searching skills to find solutions more effectively.

    • Asking my peers:
  • Seeking assistance from peers remains a challenging aspect for me, particularly in remote work settings. It's an area where I'm working to improve my comfort level.

    • Asking my facilitators:
  • On the other hand, approaching facilitators is something I'm a bit more comfortable with, though it still presents occasional challenges, especially in remote learning environments.

    • Reflection:
  • Reflection has become a cornerstone of my learning process. Recognising its benefits early on, I've wholeheartedly embraced it. Reflecting helps solidify the methods and concepts I've learned, and I intend to continue practising this valuable skill to further enhance my learning journey.
